# 18. The Experimental Frame

Later **hands-on labs** and **case studies** need a single shared frame. Without it, comparisons reproduce the same ambiguity the earlier chapters warned against. What follows is the rule set for “same task, multiple structures, read side by side.”

## Core design principle

Each lab will hold one thing stable and vary one thing deliberately.

What stays stable:

- the underlying task
- the available context
- the model or model class when possible
- the evaluation dimensions

What varies:

- the prompt structure
- the depth of constraints
- the presence or absence of definitions of done
- the framing of scope, verification, and non-goals

## Why this matters

Without a stable frame, any comparison becomes easy to misread. A better answer may have come from a better prompt, a different scope, more context, or simply luck. A useful lab must control what it can and say clearly what it cannot.

## Proposed lab unit

Each case will eventually follow the same shape:

1. one task
2. several prompt variants
3. one result per variant
4. a comparison table
5. a brief editorial reading of what changed and why

This gives the section repeatability. Readers will not just see one dramatic example. They will be able to see a pattern across cases.

## What the labs are trying to show

The labs are not trying to prove that one perfect prompt exists. They are trying to show that prompt structure changes:

- investigation depth
- stopping behavior
- hidden-problem discovery
- quality of verification
- design quality of proposed solutions

That is the real claim of this section.
