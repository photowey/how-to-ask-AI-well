# How to Use This Guide

You can read this book in order. In fact, I recommend it if you want the full argument. But you do not have to.

This guide is designed to support two kinds of reading:

- **linear reading**, where you move chapter by chapter and build the full model
- **problem-driven reading**, where you jump directly to the chapters that match the work in front of you

## If you are new to the subject

Start at the beginning and stay with the first two parts.

Part I explains why so many AI conversations produce shallow work. Part II gives you the structural elements of a strong request. If you skip those sections and jump straight into templates, you will still get value, but you will miss the deeper logic that makes the later material adaptable.

Recommended path:

1. [About This Book](/about-this-book)
2. [Why I Wrote This Book](/why-i-wrote-this-book)
3. Part I
4. Part II
5. Part III

## If you already use AI heavily

You may want to move more selectively.

If your main problem is shallow execution, read:

- Chapter 2: why “don’t give me an MVP” usually fails
- Chapter 4: goals, constraints, and definitions of done
- Chapter 8: the eight modules of a strong request
- Chapter 9: reusable prompt patterns

If your main problem is design quality, read:

- Chapter 7: change axes
- Chapter 14: designing extensible systems
- the appendices on prompt snippets and forms

If your main problem is review depth, read:

- Chapter 6: verification and evidence
- Chapter 10: anti-patterns
- Chapter 15: reviewing code for real risk

## How the chapters are built

Most chapters follow the same rhythm:

1. a central claim
2. a description of the common failure mode
3. an explanation of what better structure looks like
4. an example or scenario
5. a practical rule, pattern, or template

This is intentional. I want the book to remain readable, but I also want it to be usable under pressure. When you are in the middle of debugging a production issue or designing a plugin system, you should be able to come back to a chapter and extract something concrete.

## How to use the templates

The templates are not scripts for controlling AI. They are scaffolds for structuring work.

Use them in three passes:

1. **Copy the template**
   Start with a pattern that matches the situation.
2. **Replace abstractions with real constraints**
   Fill in the actual goal, boundaries, risks, and definition of done.
3. **Trim what does not matter**
   Better structure does not mean more words. It means more relevant structure.

## How to use the appendices

The appendices are built as a toolkit:

- **Prompt Snippets** for direct reuse
- **Fill-in Forms** for faster recurring work
- **Terminology Notes** so the book uses terms consistently

If you are building your own personal prompting workflow, these pages matter as much as the main chapters.

## A final reading instruction

Do not read this book as if it were merely about wording. That is too small.

Read it as a guide to **work design** in AI collaboration. Once you see that clearly, the rest of the book becomes simpler:

- good prompts define work
- bad prompts create ambiguity
- ambiguity invites shallow optimization
- shallow optimization feels fast until you pay for it later
